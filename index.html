Dog Emotion Classification Model

TikTok Boredom and Attention

I set out to answer whether TikTok actually “rots your brain” more than other types of content. To investigate this, I had participants watch either TikTok videos or longer-format videos and then measured changes in their boredom levels and attention spans. My main finding was that it took until the fifth day for attention scores to differ significantly between the experimental (TikTok) and control groups. Prior media use was controlled for, but consistent with previous research, it was associated with higher boredom and lower attention spans. Overall, short-term TikTok use did not seem to cause greater boredom or attention problems. However, it may have long-term effects over extended periods. If I were to continue this research, I would study TikTok usage over a longer time frame and focus on binge-watching behaviors rather than short-term exposure. CIMPT

I assisted my professor and advisor, Dr. Andy Hertel, along with four other students, in testing an intervention designed to help individuals reduce or stop cannabis use. The intervention is called the CIMP-T (Cannabis-User Identity Misfit Perception Task), adapted from the previously developed and tested DIMP-T, which was used for alcohol users. In this task, cannabis users are asked to write down negative traits they associate with typical cannabis users. They then reflect on how those traits don’t apply to themselves, which helps create a sense of identity misfit. This shift in self-perception is intended to motivate users to reduce their cannabis use. On the left is a picture of the research poster with more information.

Grandparents And Giving

My group and I set out to explore which grandparents invest the most in their grandchildren and whether there are cultural differences between individualistic and collectivistic societies. We distributed a campus-wide survey asking participants to report how much each of their four biological grandparents (mother's mother, mother's father, father's mother, and father's father) invested in them in terms of emotional closeness, perceived liking, communication, and financial support. We found that cultural background did not significantly affect grandparental investment. However, maternal grandparents—both the mother's mother and father—invested significantly more in all categories except financial support. Marijuana and Attention

As seen above, I created two cognitive games as part of a larger app project. I conducted a psychological study using one of these games to examine whether marijuana use affects working memory. Using a within-subjects design, participants played the game both sober and while inebriated. We found that participants performed significantly worse on the attention game when inebriated compared to when sober. Interestingly, the difference in performance between sober and inebriated states was smaller for frequent marijuana users—this effect approached statistical significance.
